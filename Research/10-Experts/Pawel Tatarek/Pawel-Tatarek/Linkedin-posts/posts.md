## 1) “Scaling content with AI” isn’t just outlining/drafting

- **Approx date**: 2026 (post shows “2mo”, fetched 2026-04-27)
- **Link**: `https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tatarekp_scaling-content-with-ai-can-mean-so-many-activity-7427299868936855553-PSsR`
- **Post (verbatim)**:

> "Scaling content with AI" can mean so many things. Outlining and drafting are the obvious ones, but not always the best ways to use AI for content. Here are a few other ways to use AI workflows to increase publishing velocity without sacrificing quality:
> - SERP analysis: AI is great at finding patterns and gaps in existing content (but not so good at finding unique, compelling angles).
> - Competitor research: I've built workflows that collect information about software or services from multiple sources and save them in a central database. So editors or writers don't have to start from scratch every time they work on a listicle or a comparison article.
> - Editing: AI lacks the intuition or taste of an experienced editor. Still, a sequence of well-structured workflows can enforce style guidelines, check if the H2/H3 structure is logical, ensure all sections are MECE and that they BLUF, find gaps in reasoning and evidence, and cut out fluff.
> - Search optimization: AI and organic. Give AI a checklist, and it will implement it.
> - Prioritizing pages to refresh: Based on multiple data points, not just rankings, clicks, or time since last update.
> - Content repurposing: AI can't come up with original ideas, but can identify them in existing content and turn them into social posts, newsletters, etc.
>
> None of these solve the respective problems 100% and require human supervision. But combined, they can shave off tons of time from the content creation process, allowing teams to create more. Or less but deeper and more thorough content!
>
> If you want to learn more, I described the workflows in my piece for MarketingExpertsHub. Will drop the link below.

- **Why it’s Product-Led**:
  - He anchors “AI scaling” in workflows that improve *content quality and usefulness* (editing checks, MECE/BLUF structure, evidence gaps), which is what Product-Led content needs to convert: clear problem-solving + credible proof.
  - He emphasizes listicles/comparisons and refresh prioritization — formats that can directly support product evaluation and BOFU decision-making.

## 2) AI automation only pays off at scale (and workflows are mostly testing)

- **Approx date**: 2026 (post shows “1mo”, fetched 2026-04-27)
- **Link**: `https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tatarekp_scaling-content-creation-is-probably-the-activity-7439982620811104256-5DHv`
- **Post (verbatim)**:

> Scaling content creation is probably the most common AI automation use case in marketing. Makes perfect sense. In fact, I'd say AI automation makes sense ONLY when you use it at scale.
>
> If I've never worked on a similar task before, getting the desired output takes a bit of prompt tinkering. It only makes sense if I plan to use the prompts again in the future. Otherwise, it's often quicker to do things without AI.
>
> In multistep workflows, the tinkering is on a whole new level. The experiments, testing, and debugging take hours. And for more complex workflows, days. I get the time back eventually, but only if I use it enough.
>
> I commonly tell new AI builders that its 10% building, 90% testing outputs and adjusting.

- **Why it’s Product-Led**:
  - Product-Led content programs tend to be *systems* (repeatable, measurable, continuously improved). His point maps directly: workflows should be reused and validated, not treated as one-off “AI shortcuts”.
  - The “90% testing” framing reinforces his bias toward quality control — critical when content is meant to act like a product surface (educate, qualify, and convert).
